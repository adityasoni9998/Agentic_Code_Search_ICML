\subsection{\methodname outperforms larger base and post-trained LLMs from complex scaffolds} 

Firstly, \methodname-4B and \methodname-14B achieve significant performance gains over corresponding 4B and 14B pre-trained models (Figure \ref{fig:performance_gain}) and also outperform significantly larger 32B reasoning model with an identical agent scaffold (OpenHands) for all three localization granularities and all evaluation benchmarks. In addition, \methodname-4B and \methodname-14B consistently outperforms Qwen2.5-32B Instruct model across numerous specialized scaffolds like RepoSearcher, Agentless, CoSIL, OrcaLoca, etc. for both file and function-level localization.

Despite using an extremely simple agent scaffold with simply a terminal tool, \methodname achieves significantly stronger localization performance than larger models from RepoSearcher and RepoNavigator, that leverage complex, specialized tools with programming language-specific implementation. We primarily compare with RepoNavigator as it has stronger performance over RepoSearcher, and is closer to our work in terms of dynamically predicting a flexible number of locations (as opposed the fixed top-K retrieval approach from RepoSearcher). 

Compared to the trained 7B, 14B and 32B models from RepoNavigator, \methodname-4B, despite its smaller size, achieves absolute gains of upto \textbf{16.94\%} in the file-level F1 score and \textbf{9.29\%} in the function-level F1 score on SWE-Bench Verified. On SWE-Bench Pro, \methodname-4B achieves absolute improvements of upto \textbf{12.03\%} in file-level F1 score over the RepoNavigator 7B and 14B models, and of upto \textbf{14.74\%} in function-level F1-scores over RepoNavigator's 7B, 14B and 32B models. In addition, \methodname-14B achieves superior function-level localization performance over the RepoNavigator 32B model, with an absolute improvement of \textbf{6.23\%} on SWE-Bench Verified \textbf{8.02\%} on SWE-Bench Pro. \methodname-14B achieves competitive file-localization performance on SWE-Bench Verified, with a small absolute gain of 0.82\%, whereas it underperforms RepoNavigator-32B by 3.94\% on SWE-Bench Pro.

\subsection{\methodname closes the performance gap with closed-source LLMs}Surprisingly, using a fully open-source pipeline, \methodname competes strongly against proprietary models. On SWE-Bench Verified (Table \ref{tab:swe_bench_results}), our models achieve substantial absolute gains in function-level localization. \methodname-14B exceeds the RepoNavigator scaffold powered by Claude-3.7-Sonnet and GPT-5-Chat by \textbf{8.6\%} and \textbf{9.15\%} in F1 scores respectively. Even \methodname-4B surpasses Claude-3.7-Sonnet by \textbf{5.1\%} and GPT-5 by \textbf{5.06\%} in function-level F1 scores. While the closed-source LLMs from the Claude family still outperform \methodname in terms of file-level localization, GPT-5-Chat significantly under-performs our models by \textbf{9.69\%} highlighting the competitiveness of our approach.

\subsection{\methodname as a Well-Rounded Code-Searcher}

We find that \methodname is flexible in that it is not constrained by any specific tool nor is by maximum number of items it must return. While RepoNavigator was trained to utilize the flow of code execution, it achieves higher File-level F1 on SWE-Bench Pro, but lags in the Function-level F1 compared to \methodname. We see this a potential issue, we argue that the true interest of a SWE Agent is the parts of the code that are relevant to the issue and not generally the whole file where the issue might come from. Allowing and sub-agent such as \methodname to explore unconstrained by a particular method proves beneficial in this scenario. Additionally, our method also implies data creation that is much simpler and thus easier to scale as necessary. 

Another popular approach to code localization configures the sub-agent to return $k$ number of candidate answers. A higher $k$ value has a higher recall or higher chance of returning highest number of relevant items but at the risk of also returning non-relevant ones. This poses a potential issue of distracting a SWE agent. On the other hand, reducing $k$ will alleviate this issue but at the cost of leaving out actually relevant items. In contrast, we do not configure \methodname with any $k$ and instead train it to with various cases where the correct set of items can be singular or multiple. This effectively strikes a balance between having to decide what $k$ value is appropriate.

\subsection{Evolving Behaviors during the training}

% \lscomment{Steps, or tool use, how does this change through out the training run, or the inference behaviour, does it use more or less greps compared to other actions before it settles for a final answer.}

In \autoref{fig:tool_use}, we observe that at the start of training, \methodname tries out multiple different bash commands such as \texttt{cat}, \texttt{grep}, etc. However, as training progresses, the agent quickly converges to using less command variations and after 250 steps only uses \texttt{rg} and \texttt{sed}. This is most likely due to the \texttt{rg} command being comprehensive enough as functionalities from \texttt{cat}, \texttt{find}, and \texttt{grep} can be done with \texttt{rg}. \texttt{cd} aslo sees dimishing use likely due to the the more effective use of \texttt{rg} that makes changing directory unnecessary.
